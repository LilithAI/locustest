## Why this matters and when to start

Until you're enrolled with a State Bar Council, you are legally a law graduate — not an advocate. You cannot sign a vakalatnama, appear in court, or formally hold yourself out as practising. For most litigation roles, chamber positions, and even some in-house counsel offers, enrolment is a hard prerequisite that needs to happen *before* you join.

Most students leave this to the last minute, then panic in June when they realise the State Bar Council needs documents their college issues in a 6-week cycle. Start early.

<Callout type="rule" title="The 90-day rule">
Begin assembling your enrolment file the day you write your last semester paper. Realistic timeline from final exam to enrolment certificate in hand: 60–120 days, depending on your State Bar Council's backlog.
</Callout>

This guide covers the standard pathway under the **Advocates Act, 1961** and the **Bar Council of India Rules**. State-specific procedures vary — always cross-check the current notification on your State Bar Council's website before you submit.

## The legal framework — what's actually happening

Two bodies regulate your entry into the profession.

### State Bar Council (SBC)

The State Bar Council enrols you. You apply to the SBC of the state where you intend to practise. Once enrolled, you receive:

- A **State Roll Number** (e.g. D/1234/2026 for Delhi).
- An **enrolment certificate**.
- A **Certificate of Practice (COP)**, valid initially for two years pending AIBE clearance.

You can only be enrolled with one SBC at a time. You can transfer later under Section 18 of the Advocates Act, but it's procedural friction — pick the right state the first time.

### Bar Council of India (BCI)

The BCI does not enrol you. It:

- Sets the rules for who can be enrolled (Section 24, Advocates Act).
- Conducts the **All India Bar Examination (AIBE)**, which you must clear to convert your provisional COP into a permanent one.
- Maintains the national roll.

<KeyPoints>
- You enrol with the **State** Bar Council, not BCI.
- Choose the state where you'll actually practise — transfers are a hassle.
- Enrolment is provisional until you clear AIBE within 2 years.
</KeyPoints>

## Eligibility — Section 24 in plain English

To be enrolled, you must:

- Be an **Indian citizen** (limited reciprocity for foreign nationals — separate process, not covered here).
- Be **at least 21 years old** on the date of application.
- Hold a **degree in law** from a university recognised by the BCI — either a 3-year LL.B. or an integrated 5-year programme (B.A. LL.B., B.B.A. LL.B., etc.).
- **Not be in any other full-time salaried employment** at the time of enrolment. This is the trap that catches in-house and policy job offers — see the section below.
- Have paid the prescribed enrolment fee.

### Disqualifications to know

- **Convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude** — disqualifies enrolment under Section 24A.
- **Removed or dismissed from government employment** on charges involving moral turpitude.
- Currently enrolled with another SBC (you must surrender the existing enrolment first).

<Callout type="warning" title="Full-time employment trap">
You cannot be enrolled while holding a full-time salaried non-legal job. If you've signed a corporate offer that starts before your enrolment hearing, you have three choices: (1) defer enrolment, (2) defer the job's start date, or (3) confirm in writing with the SBC that the role qualifies as legal practice (in-house counsel positions usually do, but the rules are state-dependent and changing — check current case law and your SBC's recent circulars).
</Callout>

## Fees — what it actually costs

The Advocates Act caps the statutory enrolment fee at ₹750 (₹600 to the SBC + ₹150 to BCI). In practice, **State Bar Councils have layered on welfare fund contributions, identity card fees, and library fees** that bring the real total to ₹15,000–₹40,000+ depending on the state.

The **Gaurav Kumar v. Union of India (2024)** Supreme Court ruling held that SBCs cannot charge enrolment fees beyond the statutory cap and ordered refunds going forward, but implementation is state-by-state and slow. Confirm the current fee structure with your SBC directly before paying.

<Callout type="tip" title="Always pay through traceable channels">
Demand draft or official online portal — never cash, even if asked. Keep the receipt and a photograph of the DD/payment confirmation. You will need this if you ever apply for a fee refund or transfer.
</Callout>

## State Bar Council vs BCI vs AIBE — the timeline

Here's how the pieces fit together once you graduate.

1. **Day 0 — Final exam ends.** Begin collecting documents.
2. **Day 30–45 — College issues provisional degree, migration certificate, character certificate.** Chase your administrative office weekly.
3. **Day 45–60 — Submit enrolment application to your SBC** with full file, fees, advocate verification, and affidavits.
4. **Day 60–90 — SBC scrutiny + enrolment hearing.** Some states call you in person; others process by file. You receive your **provisional Certificate of Practice** and State Roll Number.
5. **Day 90+ — You are now legally an advocate** (provisionally). You can sign vakalatnamas and appear in court.
6. **Within 2 years — Clear the AIBE.** Held twice a year by BCI. Pass mark is currently 40% (general) / 35% (reserved). Open-book, multiple-choice. You receive a **Certificate of Practice (permanent)**.

If you don't clear AIBE within 2 years of enrolment, your COP can be suspended — though in practice BCI has been lenient and the rules have been litigated repeatedly. Don't rely on leniency. Sit AIBE in the first attempt after enrolment.

<Callout type="rule" title="AIBE is open-book — but not easy">
Most students underestimate it. The pass rate hovers around 60–70%. Bareacts, commentaries, and printed notes are allowed in the hall. Take the syllabus seriously, do at least 10 past papers, and tab your bareacts before the exam.
</Callout>

## Picking the right State Bar Council

You can only enrol with one SBC at a time. Choose where you'll spend your first 3–5 years of practice.

### Considerations

- **Where are your chambers / firm / clients?** Litigation work is overwhelmingly local. Enrol where you'll appear.
- **Domicile requirements.** A few SBCs (Delhi historically, parts of Maharashtra) have informal domicile preferences and slower processing for outsiders. Check current practice.
- **Fee structure.** Variation is significant — Maharashtra and Karnataka have historically been higher; smaller states cheaper.
- **Processing speed.** Delhi and Maharashtra have significant backlogs; smaller SBCs often process faster.
- **Transfer cost.** Transferring to another SBC under Section 18 is allowed but takes 4–8 months and a fresh fee. Avoid if possible.

<PullQuote attribution="Common advice from senior advocates">
Enrol where you'll work. Don't enrol in your home state for sentimental reasons if you're moving to Delhi for a litigation job in two months — you'll just spend a year getting the transfer done.
</PullQuote>

## What to do while you wait

The 60–120 day window between application and enrolment is dead time if you let it be. Don't.

- **Start AIBE prep immediately.** You'll be eligible to sit it as soon as you're enrolled. Tab your bareacts now.
- **Begin chamber readings or shadowing.** Even unenrolled, you can sit in chambers, take notes, and read briefs as a junior — the practice starts before the certificate arrives.
- **Build your CV's "post-graduation" line.** Publications, a self-led research project, or a study group on a specific area. Don't appear in the gap with nothing to show.
- **Get your professional kit.** Black coat, white bands, white shirt, court file, plain visiting cards. Court tailors are slow; order early.

## Post-enrolment: the first 90 days

You now have a State Roll Number. The work begins.

- **Get your photo identity card** issued by the SBC. You need this to enter most courts.
- **Register with the local bar association** of the court complex where you'll appear (e.g. Delhi High Court Bar Association, separately from your SBC enrolment).
- **Open a separate bank account** for any client receipts — even as a junior, this is good hygiene from day one.
- **Sign up for AIBE the next available cycle.** Don't push it.
- **Get familiar with e-filing portals** for the courts you'll practise in. Most Indian high courts now have mandatory e-filing for fresh matters.

<KeyPoints>
- Enrolment is with your **State** Bar Council, not BCI. Choose the state where you'll practise.
- Realistic timeline: **60–120 days** from final exam to enrolment certificate.
- The **migration certificate** and **advocate verification** are the two most-delayed items. Start both in month 1.
- **AIBE within 2 years** of enrolment, or your COP can be suspended.
- Statutory fee is ₹750. Real cost is ₹15k–₹40k+ depending on state — confirm before paying.
- You cannot hold a non-legal full-time salaried job while enrolled.
</KeyPoints>
