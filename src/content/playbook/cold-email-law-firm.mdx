## Why most cold emails fail

You're not getting replies because your email looks like every other one in the partner's inbox. Generic salutation, vague interest in "your esteemed firm," resume pasted at the bottom. Delete.

Partners and senior associates get 30–80 cold emails a week. They scan the subject line, the first sentence, and the signature. If nothing pulls them in within five seconds, you're archived.

<Callout type="rule" title="The five-second rule">
If a busy partner can't tell who you are, why you're writing, and what you want within five seconds, your email is dead on arrival.
</Callout>

The good news: most students send the same template. Doing the basics right puts you in the top 10% immediately.

## Finding the right contact

Sending to `info@firm.com` or `careers@firm.com` is a black hole. You need a real human — ideally someone who'd actually want to mentor an intern.

### Where to look

- **The firm's website team page** — find associates and senior associates in the practice area you care about. Avoid managing partners; they almost never read intern email.
- **LinkedIn** — search "[Firm Name] associate" and filter by current company. Look at their posts; people who post are more likely to respond.
- **Bar Council India directory** — for chambers and senior advocates, this is your primary source.
- **Recent judgments** — if a junior advocate appeared in a notable matter, their name is in the cause title. Look them up.

For the email address itself: most Indian firms use `firstname.lastname@firm.com` or `firstinitiallastname@firm.com`. Use a free email-finder tool, or just guess and send a test.

<Callout type="tip" title="Don't email more than two people at the same firm">
If both reply, you look organised. If both ignore you, you've burned the firm. Pick the best-fit contact and commit.
</Callout>

## Writing the subject line

The subject line is the entire email. Get it wrong, nothing else matters.

### What works

- `Internship application — [Your Name], [Your College], [Year]`
- `Cold email from a 4th-year [College] student interested in IP`
- `[Mutual connection] suggested I reach out — internship enquiry`

### What kills your email

- `Internship` (one word, no context)
- `Application for the post of Intern at your esteemed firm` (formal Victorian)
- `URGENT — Internship` (you're not urgent to them)
- Anything with "kindly do the needful"

## The email structure

Four short paragraphs. Total length under 180 words. If you can't fit it in 180 words, you don't know what you're saying.

1. **Who you are, in one line.** Year, college, degree, and an optional one-line credibility marker — a moot, a publication, a prior internship.
2. **Why this firm, specifically.** Reference a recent matter, a partner's article, or a practice area the firm is known for. This is where 95% of cold emails fail. Generic "I'm impressed by your work" gets deleted.
3. **What you want.** Specific dates, specific duration, specific area. *"I'm writing to enquire about a 4-week internship in your IP team between 15 May and 15 June 2026."*
4. **Soft close + signature.** Thank them, attach your CV, and sign off with phone and LinkedIn.

## Following up

If you don't hear back in 7 business days, send one follow-up. One. Not three.

The follow-up is two sentences:

- Reference the original email + date.
- Ask if they've had a chance to consider it.

If you don't hear back after the follow-up, move on. Don't email a third time. Don't email a different person at the same firm hoping for a different answer. Move on professionally — you may need this firm in two years.

<KeyPoints>
- Subject lines decide whether you get read. Spend 5 minutes on it.
- Specificity beats flattery. Reference one real thing about the firm.
- 180 words. Four paragraphs. One follow-up. Then move on.
- Track every email you send so you don't double-email or lose track.
</KeyPoints>
