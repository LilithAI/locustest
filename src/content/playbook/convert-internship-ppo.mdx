## What firms look for in interns

A pre-placement offer (PPO) is not a reward for being clever. It's a bet by the firm that you'll be a good associate two years from now. Partners are pattern-matching against the associates who've worked out and the ones who haven't.

The traits they look for, in order of weight:

1. **Reliability.** You finish what you start. You meet deadlines. You don't disappear.
2. **Judgment.** You know what to escalate and what to handle. You don't bring problems without proposed solutions.
3. **Writing.** Your drafts need light editing, not rewriting.
4. **Temperament.** People want to share an office with you at 11 PM on a deal night.

Notice what's not on this list: knowing the most case law, having the best CGPA, being from the most prestigious college. These get you the internship. They don't get you the PPO.

The interns who get PPOs are the ones I find myself wanting to give more work to. It's that simple.
</PullQuote>

## The visibility strategy

You can be the best intern in the firm and still not get a PPO if no decision-maker knows it. Visibility is not about being loud — it's about your work landing in front of the right people.

How to do this without being annoying:

- **Own the output.** When you submit work, attach your name to it. Sign the email, put your initials on the document, take credit cleanly.
- **Default to "I'll take it."** When the team needs a volunteer for a small task, raise your hand. Three of these over six weeks and you become "the reliable one."
- **Send a Friday wrap-up email** to the associate you primarily report to: 5 bullets on what you did this week, what's pending, what you'll do next week. Most interns don't do this. The ones who do get remembered.
- **Be physically present.** Show up early. Stay until the team's senior leaves. Working from a hot desk in the corridor is fine if it gets you in the room.

<Callout type="warning" title="Do not skip team events">
The post-work drinks, the team lunch, the office cricket match. These are where partners decide who they like working with. "I'm an introvert" is not a valid excuse if you want the offer.
</Callout>

## Asking for feedback

Most interns leave without ever asking how they're doing. Then they're surprised when the PPO doesn't come.

Three feedback moments to engineer:

**Week 2 check-in.** "I want to make sure I'm being useful. Is there anything I should be doing differently?" Short, direct, asked once. Note what they say. Fix it.

**Mid-internship informal review.** Around the halfway point, ask the associate you work with most: "If I wanted to be considered for an offer here, what should I focus on in the second half?" This question explicitly puts you in the running.

**Exit conversation.** In your last week, request 15 minutes with the partner. "I've really enjoyed working here and I'd love your honest assessment — what would I need to demonstrate to be considered for an offer in [Year]?"

That last question is the magic one. You're not asking for an offer. You're asking what an offer would require. Hard to refuse.

## The follow-up timeline

You walked out with the partner saying "we'll be in touch." Now what.

**Week 1 after leaving.** Send a thank-you email to the partner and the associate you worked with most. Specific, two paragraphs, no ask.

**Week 4.** Send the associate one short message — share an article or judgment you saw that's relevant to a matter you worked on. No ask. Just staying alive in their inbox.

**Month 3.** Send a meaningful update — a moot result, a publication, a clerkship. One paragraph. End with "happy to come back for another stint if useful — let me know."

**Month 6.** If you're in the city, suggest coffee. Not a meeting — coffee. 30 minutes.

This rhythm — thank, share, update, meet — keeps you in their head without ever being needy.

## Writing the PPO request

When the time comes (usually 6–9 months after your internship, sometimes later), you write *the* email. This is not the time to be cute.

<KeyPoints>
- The PPO is decided during the internship. Behave accordingly from day one.
- Visibility is not loudness. It's making your work land in front of decision-makers.
- Engineer three feedback moments — week 2, midpoint, exit.
- Stay in touch on a 1 → 4 → 12 → 24 week rhythm. Then ask.
</KeyPoints>
