## Day one — what actually happens

You'll show up at 9:30 or 10. Someone at reception will look mildly surprised. After 20–40 minutes of waiting, an associate will collect you, walk you to a desk (or sometimes a meeting room), and hand you something to read. That's day one.

Don't expect:

- A formal onboarding session
- An assigned mentor
- A welcome lunch
- Anyone explaining the firm structure

Do expect:

- Confusion about who you report to
- A pile of judgments to read for "context"
- Long stretches of silence punctuated by sudden urgent tasks

This is normal. Internships in Indian firms are not graduate programs. You learn by absorbing.

<Callout type="tip" title="Bring your own setup">
Notebook, pen, laptop charger, water bottle, and a printed copy of your CV. Wear formal clothes even if the firm is "smart casual" — you can dress down on day three once you read the room.
</Callout>

## Types of work you'll be given

Your first week is mostly:

- **Reading judgments** to understand a matter the team is working on. You're not expected to produce anything yet — but take notes, because you'll be asked questions.
- **Compiling case lists** — pulling together a list of cases on a particular point of law. Use Manupatra, SCC Online, and Indian Kanoon. Triple-check citations.
- **Document indexing** — labelling and ordering exhibits for a paperbook or compilation. Tedious but mistakes are very visible.

By week two, if you've done the above without issues, you'll start getting:

- **Research notes** — a 2–4 page summary on a specific legal question
- **Drafting tasks** — sections of pleadings, written submissions, or contract clauses
- **Attending court** — note-taking from a hearing, sometimes carrying files

The mistake students make is treating week-one work as beneath them. Don't. The associate is testing whether you can do the boring stuff carefully before they trust you with the interesting stuff.

## How to ask questions properly

There's a right and wrong way to ask questions in a law firm.

**Wrong:** Walking up to an associate's desk every time you hit a small obstacle.

**Right:** Batch your questions. Try to solve the problem yourself first. When you do ask, use this structure:

1. What you were asked to do
2. What you've already tried
3. The specific thing you're stuck on
4. Your best guess at the answer

<EmailBlock
  subject="Quick question on the Tata Sons matter — research note"
  body={`Hi Ravi,

For the research note on Section 9 vs Section 17 of the Arbitration Act, I've found 6 relevant judgments and drafted a 2-page comparison.

I'm unsure whether to include the recent Delhi HC order in [Case Name] — it's strictly obiter on this point but the reasoning seems directly relevant. My instinct is to include it in a footnote rather than the main text.

Happy to walk over and discuss if easier. No rush — I'll keep working on the rest.

Thanks,
[Your Name]`}
/>

This shows respect for the associate's time and demonstrates that you've actually tried.

## Tracking your work

Keep a daily log. Not for the firm — for you. Every day, write down:

- Matters you worked on (case name, partner, what you did)
- Areas of law you touched
- New people you met
- Things you didn't know that you now know

This log becomes your CV bullets, your conversation starters at future internships, and your evidence when partners ask "so what have you been working on?" three weeks in.

<Callout type="rule" title="Confidentiality is non-negotiable">
Never put client names, deal terms, or specific facts in your personal notes outside the office network. Use case codes you make up. The day a partner thinks you might leak something is the day your career at that firm ends.
</Callout>

## End-of-internship checklist

The last week is more important than the first. Do these things before you walk out:

<Checklist items={[
  "Submit a clean handover note for any incomplete work — what's done, what's pending, where files are saved.",
  "Send a thank-you email to the partner and the associates you worked with most closely. Specific, not generic.",
  "Ask for an internship certificate, on letterhead, with your name spelled correctly. Get it before you leave — chasing it later is hard.",
  "Connect with the team on LinkedIn. Don't message them about future opportunities yet — just connect.",
  "Update your CV that night while details are fresh. Two lines per matter, action verbs, no jargon nobody outside the firm understands.",
]} />

Two months later, send a short update email to the associate who gave you the most work. Not asking for anything — just a "here's what I've been doing since." That single email is what turns a one-time internship into a two-year relationship.
