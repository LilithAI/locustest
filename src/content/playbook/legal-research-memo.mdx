## What a memo is and isn't

A legal research memo is not an essay. It's not a literature review. It's not your chance to demonstrate everything you know about an area of law.

A memo is a **decision-support document**. A senior person needs to make a call — file or not, accept the term sheet or push back, settle or litigate — and they need a clear, honest answer with the legal reasoning behind it.

Three things a good memo must do:

1. State the question precisely
2. Give the answer up front
3. Show your reasoning so the reader can pressure-test it

If your memo doesn't do all three, you've written something else.

<Callout type="warning" title="Length is not quality">
A 12-page memo answering a question that needed 3 pages tells the partner you can't synthesise. Cut everything that isn't load-bearing.
</Callout>

## Structure: IRAC explained

IRAC — Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion — is the spine of every legal memo. Not because law school says so, but because it mirrors how lawyers actually think.

**Issue.** One sentence. Sharp. "Whether a non-compete clause restraining employment for 18 months post-termination is enforceable under Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act."

Vague issues lead to vague memos. If you can't write the issue in one sentence, you don't yet understand the question.

**Rule.** The applicable law. Statute first, then leading judgments. Quote sparingly — paraphrase precisely.

**Application.** This is where 90% of student memos collapse. You apply the rule to *these specific facts*. Not facts in the abstract. Not facts from a similar case. The actual facts in front of you.

**Conclusion.** A clear answer. "On the facts presented, the clause is likely unenforceable, subject to the qualifications in Part IV below." Not "it depends" with no qualification.

<KeyPoints>
- Issue: one sentence. Be ruthless.
- Rule: statute, then case law. Cite, don't quote.
- Application: this is where the partner judges your thinking.
- Conclusion: take a position. Hedge with reasoning, not with vibes.
</KeyPoints>

## Research methodology

Before you start writing, you research. Before you research, you plan.

**Step 1 — Decompose the issue.** Most legal questions have 2–4 sub-issues hiding inside them. List them.

**Step 2 — Map the sources.** Statute → Rules/Regulations → Supreme Court → relevant High Court → tribunal/commission → leading commentary.

**Step 3 — Search systematically.** Manupatra, SCC Online, Indian Kanoon. Use the actual section number, not paraphrases. Read headnotes, then full judgments only for the cases that matter.

**Step 4 — Check currency.** Has the statute been amended? Has the leading judgment been overruled? Has a Constitution Bench taken a different view? You don't want to discover this from the partner.

<Callout type="tip" title="Build a research log">
Open a separate doc. For every search, note: query, database, date, top 3 results. Halfway through the memo when you can't find that one judgment, you'll thank yourself.
</Callout>

## Writing style and tone

Memo prose is not court prose. No "humbly submitted." No "it is most respectfully prayed." No "the Hon'ble Court."

What you want:

- **Short sentences.** Average under 20 words.
- **Active voice.** "The Court held" not "It was held by the Court."
- **Concrete words.** "The clause restrains for 18 months" not "the clause is of considerable temporal extent."
- **No throat-clearing.** Cut every "It is pertinent to note that" and "It is worth highlighting that."
- **Define abbreviations once.** ICA for Indian Contract Act, then use it.

A good test: read your memo aloud. If you stumble or run out of breath, your sentences are too long.

## Common mistakes

**Citing without reading.** Every citation in your memo must come from a judgment you've actually read. Partners *will* ask "so what did the court say in para 47?" If you can't answer, you'll never be trusted again.

**Copy-pasting headnotes.** Manupatra and SCC headnotes are not always accurate, and partners can spot them instantly. Read the judgment. Write your own one-line summary.

**Missing the question.** You researched a related question and answered that one instead. Always re-read the partner's original instruction before sending.

**No counter-argument.** Real partners want to know what the other side will say. A memo that only argues one way is half-finished. Add a "Counter-arguments and risks" section even if it's just two paragraphs.

**Bad formatting.** Inconsistent fonts, broken numbering, footnotes pointing to the wrong sources. The partner's first impression of your work is visual. Get it right.
