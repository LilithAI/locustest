## Why most law students network wrong

Most law students treat networking like a transaction: add a partner on LinkedIn, send a copy-pasted "I am keen to intern under your esteemed guidance" message, wait for an internship offer, get nothing, repeat. That is not networking. That is cold begging with extra steps.

Real networking is the slow accumulation of people who know who you are, what you're good at, and would say yes to a 15-minute call when you ask. It pays out 18 months later — not next week.

<Callout type="rule" title="The only rule that matters">
Networking is the practice of being useful to people before you need anything from them. If your first message is an ask, you've already lost.
</Callout>

The students who end up with strong PPOs, clerkships, and chamber spots almost never "applied cold" for them. Someone vouched. Your job for the next three to five years is to build the small circle of people willing to vouch.

## The two-degree rule

You don't need to know partners. You need to know the people partners trust — usually senior associates, fifth-year associates, and recently-graduated alumni from your college.

A 2025 fifth-year at a Tier-1 firm has:

- The partner's ear during intern allocation meetings.
- A live memory of how the application game actually works.
- An incentive to refer good juniors (it makes them look good).

A managing partner has none of those incentives toward you and 200 emails a week. Stop emailing managing partners.

<KeyPoints>
- Target people 3–7 years senior to you. They have access and time.
- One warm intro from a fifth-year beats ten cold emails to partners.
- Your alumni network is the most underused asset in Indian legal education.
</KeyPoints>

## LinkedIn done properly

LinkedIn is the single highest-leverage networking tool for an Indian law student, and 90% of you are using it as a passive resume host. Fix the basics first.

### Your profile

- **Headline:** Not "Law Student at XYZ." Try `4th-year B.A. LL.B. | Interested in competition law & tech regulation | NLU Jodhpur`. Specificity signals seriousness.
- **About section:** Three short paragraphs — what you study, what you've worked on, what you're looking for. No "passionate about justice" filler.
- **Featured:** Pin two things — a publication, a moot result, a writing sample, or a project. This is the first thing anyone clicks.
- **Experience:** Every internship gets 2–3 bullets describing actual work, not the firm's marketing line. *"Drafted 4 written submissions in commercial arbitrations under [Counsel's name]."*

### Your activity

The single biggest unlock: **post your own thinking, once every 2–3 weeks**.

- A 200-word take on a recent judgment.
- A breakdown of something you learned in an internship (no client details).
- A book or paper recommendation with one paragraph on why.

You are not trying to go viral. You're trying to make sure that when a senior associate googles you, they find a thinking lawyer instead of a blank profile.

<Callout type="tip" title="The 5-minute LinkedIn habit">
Every weekday, open LinkedIn for 5 minutes. Comment thoughtfully on one post by someone you'd like to know. Not "Insightful!" — an actual sentence engaging with the argument. Do this for six months and your name becomes familiar to 20–30 people in the profession.
</Callout>

## Reaching out to seniors

The cold DM most students send: *"Hello sir/ma'am, I am a law student and I am very keen to intern under your esteemed guidance. Kindly do the needful."* This dies in the inbox.

The DM that works has four parts and stays under 90 words.

1. **One specific reason you're writing to them**, not anyone else. A post they wrote, a matter they argued, a paper they published.
2. **One line about you** — year, college, area of interest. That's it.
3. **A small, specific ask.** Not "internship." A 15-minute call. A pointer to a resource. An opinion on a question.
4. **An exit clause.** *"Completely understand if you don't have time."*

<EmailBlock
  to="LinkedIn DM — Senior Associate, IP team"
  subject="Quick question on your DHC injunction post"
  body={`Hi Aarav,

Your post last week on the Delhi HC's interim injunction framework in pharma matters was the clearest explanation of the post-Wockhardt position I've read. I'm a third-year at GNLU writing my seminar paper on Section 3(d) and patent linkage.

Would you have 15 minutes in the next two weeks for a call? I have two specific questions on how counsel approach prima facie case construction in pre-grant oppositions, and I'd value your view.

Completely understand if your schedule doesn't allow it.

Thanks for the post — it's already in my reading list for juniors.

Aanya`}
/>

Notice: no internship ask, no resume attached, no "kindly do the needful." The internship conversation happens 6 months later, after you've stayed in light contact and they remember you as the student with thoughtful questions.

## In-person: where students actually meet seniors

LinkedIn opens doors. In-person closes them. The Indian legal profession still runs on faces, and the students who show up have a 5x advantage.

### Worth your time

- **NLS / NALSAR / NLU Delhi fests and conferences.** Even if you don't go to those colleges. Register, pay the fee, attend the panels, hang back after to talk to speakers. Most speakers stay 10–15 minutes after.
- **Bar Association events** in your city. State Bar Council CLEs are open to law students for free or a token fee. Walk in, sit at the back, talk to people during tea.
- **Court canteens** — particularly Delhi HC, Bombay HC, Madras HC. Spend a vacation interning with a counsel and you will eat lunch next to people you should know. This is the highest-density legal networking environment in India and almost no student uses it deliberately.
- **Book launches and lecture series** at NLUs, Indian Law Institute, NUJS, and chambers. Free, low-pressure, and the audience is exactly the cohort you want.

### Don't bother

- **Generic "law student summits"** organised by unknown bodies. The attendees are other law students. You are not networking up.
- **Anything that asks you to pay a "delegate fee" of ₹3,000+** for a one-day virtual event. Those are revenue plays, not communities.

<Callout type="warning" title="The conference mistake">
Do not collect 40 LinkedIn connections at a conference and never speak to any of them again. Pick three people. Have real conversations. Follow up by Tuesday with a specific reference to what you discussed. Quality over quantity, every time.
</Callout>

## The first conversation

You've gotten the call or the coffee. You have 15–20 minutes. Most students burn it by either (a) interviewing themselves or (b) silently waiting for the senior to lead.

Run the call like a structured interview — but you're the interviewer.

- **Opening (2 min):** Thank them, restate the specific reason you reached out, and frame what you want to learn.
- **Their story (5 min):** "Walk me through how you ended up in [their practice area]. What surprised you?" People love telling this and it tells you more than any firm brochure.
- **Your real questions (5 min):** Two or three substantive questions you've prepared. Not "how do I get into your firm." Try: *"How do you decide which interns to keep in touch with?"* or *"What does a fifth-year associate actually spend their day on?"*
- **Soft close (3 min):** Ask for one referral or resource. *"Is there one person you'd suggest I also speak to?"* or *"What's the one paper you'd want every junior to have read?"*
- **Thank you within 24 hours.** Email, not DM. Reference one specific thing they said.

## Maintaining relationships without being annoying

This is where every student fails. You have one good call, and then you go silent for 14 months until you need an internship recommendation. They've forgotten you exist.

The maintenance system that works:

<Checklist
  title="Quarterly contact loop"
  items={[
    "Month 1: Thank-you note + one follow-up question from your call.",
    "Month 4: Share something genuinely useful — a paper, an article, a podcast — with one line on why it reminded you of them.",
    "Month 7: Update them on something you've done. \"I ended up writing that seminar paper on Section 3(d). Attaching it in case it's useful.\"",
    "Month 10: A specific, time-bound ask if you have one. Or just a wish on Diwali / New Year with a personal line.",
  ]}
/>

Four touches a year, each adding value. After 18 months they remember your name and have a positive impression. After 36 months they're vouching for you.

<PullQuote attribution="A partner at a Tier-1 Mumbai firm, on intern referrals">
The interns we end up keeping are almost never the ones who applied through the website. They're the ones a senior associate has been quietly tracking for two years.
</PullQuote>

## What to never do

- **Mass-DM 50 partners on LinkedIn the week before vacation.** It is visible from a kilometre away and partners talk to each other.
- **Ask for an internship in the first message.** Always.
- **"Bump"** your unanswered cold email three times. One follow-up. Then leave it.
- **Connect and immediately pitch.** Wait. Engage with their content for two months first.
- **Pretend you've read something you haven't.** Senior lawyers read carefully. They will catch you in the first follow-up question.
- **Treat juniors badly.** Today's third-year is your senior associate in five years. Reputations in the Indian bar are smaller and longer than you think.
