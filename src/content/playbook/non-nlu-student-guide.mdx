## Reframing the disadvantage

If you're at a non-NLU, you've probably been told some version of this: "Top firms only recruit from NLUs." That's not actually true — but it is true that the recruitment pipeline is built around NLUs, which means you have to build your own pipeline.

NLU students get on-campus recruitment, alumni networks pre-built into Slack groups, and partners who know the curriculum. You don't get any of that by default. So you stop competing on those terms and start competing on the things that actually matter to a hiring partner: writing, work ethic, and proof you can do the work.

<PullQuote attribution="Senior Associate, Tier-1 Mumbai firm">
We hire interns based on whether they can draft a memo we don't have to rewrite. Where they studied is a tiebreaker, not a filter.
</PullQuote>

## What firms actually look for

In a CV pile of 200, the partner is filtering on three things:

1. **Can this person write?** A writing sample, a published article, a moot brief — anything that proves you can construct an argument.
2. **Has this person done legal work before?** Even a 4-week chamber internship counts. Empty CV = unknown risk.
3. **Will this person actually show up and finish what they start?** Long-term commitments visible on the CV — debate society for 3 years, journal editorial board, sustained moot participation — signal reliability.

Notice what's not on this list: ranking of college, CGPA above 7.5, number of certificates from random online courses.

## Building your portfolio

You need three things on your CV that an NLU student probably has by default. Build each one deliberately.

<Checklist items={[
  "One writing sample. 800–1,500 words. A case comment, a memo, a published article. Hosted on a public link (Medium, SSRN, Google Drive view link).",
  "Two prior internships. Even unpaid chamber work with a District Court advocate counts. Sequencing matters — show progression.",
  "One sustained extracurricular. Moot court team, law journal, legal aid clinic. Three years on it beats six different things in one year.",
  "A LinkedIn profile that doesn't embarrass you. Headshot, headline, summary, education, every internship described in 2 lines.",
]} />

If your CV doesn't have these, fix that before you send a single application.

## Direct application strategy

Without on-campus recruitment, every application is a cold approach. That's actually an advantage if you do it right — you get to choose the firm, the team, the partner.

**The volume game is wrong.** Sending 200 emails to `info@firm.com` will get you nothing. Sending 25 carefully personalised emails to specific associates in specific teams will get you 3–5 responses.

**Pick your firms in three tiers:**

- **Tier 1 (5 firms):** stretch picks. Top firms, top chambers. You probably won't get in this cycle but you start the relationship.
- **Tier 2 (10 firms):** strong-fit firms. Mid-tier, regional leaders, specialised boutiques. This is your real target.
- **Tier 3 (10 firms):** safety. Smaller firms, junior counsels. You will get an offer here.

Apply to all three tiers in parallel. Do not "wait to hear back" before sending the next batch.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't apply through the form unless that's the only option">
Forms go into a queue. A cold email to a real associate gets a real reply. If the firm has both, do both.
</Callout>

## Making it stick

Once you land the internship, the goal shifts from "getting in" to "being remembered." This is where non-NLU students often outperform — you have something to prove and you show up like you mean it.

The behaviours that get you remembered:

- Submit drafts before the deadline, not at it.
- Ask one good question per week, not five mediocre ones per day.
- Send a clean, structured handover note on your last day.
- Stay in touch every 4–6 months. Not asking for anything — just an update.

Two cycles of this and your "I went to a non-NLU" is irrelevant. People hire who they remember.

<KeyPoints>
- The pipeline isn't built for you. Build your own.
- Three things on your CV: writing sample, two internships, one sustained extracurricular.
- 25 personalised emails beat 200 generic ones.
- Show up like you mean it. People hire who they remember.
</KeyPoints>
